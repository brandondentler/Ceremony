FORDPass

Redesigning the FORDPass app to fit their customer's demographics

Fall 2019 (5 months, August–December 2019)

Project Brief

During the Fall 2019 semester, my team and I had the opportunity to work on a project sponsored by UEGroup and Ford Motor Company as student collaborators for Purdue University's Experience Studio. We identified a section of FordPass, a mobile car application, that would enable it to capture or recreate a positive feeling that we experience when using our favorite app.

FordPass is Ford's "all in one" mobile car application that allows users to monitor and control their vehicle. Several features include lock and unlock, remote start, map of nearby locations, vehicle details, schedule start, and schedule maintenance.

My Role

I was part of a team of six UX Design students. I helped our team familiarize ourselves on topics relevant to our project through extensive online research, including studying current user thoughts on FORDPass and problem areas with their exisiting solutions. In doing so, I established the finalized problem statement approved by sponsors as well as built a persona that accurately reflected the needs of our user group. I also played a big part in the project idea and wireframes.

Problem

The mission going into this collaboration was...

The need is to design a mobile application that will help drivers and passengers deal with emergencies such as an accident, a breakdown, etc.”

Throughout this collaboration, our scope changed a multitude of times based on what was found throughout the project's process.

Scope

Redesign for user base found through research
After our final scope, our goal was to redesign the home screen for the users we found during research throughout. We are redesigning the home page for a user group of middle-aged car owners.

Characteristic Model

This is a research technique that is used in early stages of understanding a concept and what exactly we are designing for. As this is a very unknown stage of the design process, this model serves as a representation of our user base’s deepest desires for the product. Users might ask, “What can I do with this thing? How can it improve my life? How will it make me feel? Will I continue using it?” The answers to these questions are the ideas that guide this model.

After learning how to properly build the characteristics model, it became time for us to create one for own. With what we came up with, we have been able to understand our conceptual implementation for FordPass through a lens that directs and guides our imagined features, interactions, visual presentations, and technical interactions. Everything we design can be referenced back to this model two ensure that what we come up with down the line will still be aligned properly towards the ideals that we value, and then, as a result, our implemented designs will be successful tuned to apps that inspire us, so that FordPass in turn will benefit.


Research


Feature Audit

We conducted a feature audit in order to understand the capabilities that exist within FordPass. We analyzed the app’s information architecture in order to gain a better understanding of each feature in context to the others, to identify gaps in the user journey, and to seek areas of design opportunity.

We conducted a feature audit to see the features that FordPass currently offers, and to see the qualities that are exhibited within the app. By doing so, we aimed to identify opportunities for improvement and ways in which a new idea could be incorporated within the app.

  • Move Section
    • The move section is the homepage of FordPass where users are greeted with a photo of their car. The remote start and lock/ unlock actions are found at the top. When a user presses Vehicle Details, they are able to see their fuel levels, tire pressure, and other car characteristics. The cards shown at the bottom include smart tips and the current weather.
  • Find Section
    • The find section allows users to search for various destinations on the map. These search options include fuel, charging, parking, dealers, collision repair, food, coffee, entertainment, travel, money, shopping, health, and recreation.
  • Guides Section
    • The guides section acts as a guide to using the FordPass app. Users can look up things that they’re wondering how to find or use within the app. The buttons at the top (chat, call, email) connect users with Ford representatives to answer their questions that may not have been answered by looking through the guides. The main topics in which users can choose from include Popular, Add Dealer, Vehicle Controls, Account, Fuel, Parking, Ford Pay, FordPass Rewards, Destinations, and Ford Credit.
  • My Account Section
    • The account section provides users with more detailed information regarding their rewards, credit, wallet, messages, settings, and permissions. They can see the rewards they have earned, which vary by dealership. The message center includes alerts and messages that they are getting from the app itself.
Information Architecture

Our goal for examining the information architecture of FordPass was to explore and map out the content currently present in the app. By doing so, we could see how a new idea based on emotion could fit into the existing architecture.

Insights
  • The "Guides" section was dense and included many repeated topics for directions
  • Organization was unstructured and confusing with no consistency between screens
  • Room for elaboration within "Maps"
  • Home screen may provide opportunities for an addition

Ideation

With advice from our sponsor to save ideation for when we felt we really had the knowledge and understanding of the app and its users, we conducted several rounds of ideation after conducing our extensive forms of research. From starting off broad, to focusing our ideation on the insights found from research, we were able to feel confident that what we were designing truly fit the needs of our users.

Initial Sketching

When initially sketching, we wanted to apply our knowledge gained from research and generate a multitude of different ideas. Each member was tasked with sketching 8-10 different ideas. After presenting them, we had a better understanding of where we wanted to go.

After generating multiple ideas, we singled out the features we liked most. Some of those include:

• Scheduled Start
• Climate Control
• Lock, Unlock, Start Engine (centered)

We chose some of our favorite sketches and presented them during a sponsor meeting to get a better understanding of where we were at and if we were on the right track. After that meeting we did further ideation and sketching,

As a team, we started to feel confident with our ideas and what we were generating. After gaining all of the knowledge and ideas generated in the past rounds of sketching, we had a general idea of what we wanted to do. We were to generate another 8-10 sketches. From here, we would again find our favorite ideas and create a frankensketch.

FrankenSketch

Our frankensketch (which is a consolidated mashup of everyone’s ideas) includes a representation of what we thought as a group needed to be included in a redesign for the homepage. We created this sketch to help us create lo-fidelity mockups with a consistent layout, but with different ways of displaying the features. The pages generated can be seen on the next slide.

Reflection

This project has been very different from others I have done. I learned how to deal with many scope shifts and learned how to look at design through emotion. The project contained many different layers and phases. Dealing with chaos and uncertainty, and understanding how to keep moving forward, exploring and creating in the midst of ambiguity was a vital experience. I quite enjoyed the creation of our desirability model. Knowing that I was working towards evoking a certain emotion in our user group generated a focus in my ideation. This project taught us to be more flexible and adaptable. It was difficult to think about pivoting halfway into the semester, but we were able to gain a new learning experience.
